Worksite Autonomous Vision Engine

WAVE

Continuous, headless computer vision for worksites.

WAVE perceives; humans decide. WAVE outputs are evidence, not verdicts. Where PROBATIO answers "is this package right?" WAVE answers "what's happening on the worksite right now?"

The capability, plainly

Pixels in. Structured events out.

WAVE is a vision pipeline, not a dashboard. It ingests imagery from the field and produces structured, signed observations that flow into the substrate. No human watches WAVE. WAVE feeds the humans who do.

Inputs · what WAVE ingests

Worksite imagery, from any source

  • Drone capture. Aerial passes from DJI, Skydio, and custom UAS payloads. Raw frames or pre-stitched orthos.
  • Fixed cameras. RTSP, ONVIF, and HTTP MJPEG streams from on-site PTZ and bullet cameras already in place.
  • Mobile uploads. Phone-camera evidence captured by field crews and pushed through a Thunderblock-bound app.
  • Batch packages. ZIP bundles, S3 prefixes, or shared drives — backfilled in arrears against historical sites.
Outputs · what WAVE produces

Structured observations, sealed as events

  • Object detections. Bounding boxes with class labels and confidence scores — cabinets, routers, cables, meters, vehicles, signage.
  • OCR strings. Text read from the image, tied to the bounding-box region it was extracted from.
  • Scene labels. Whole-image classifications with confidence — daylight, weather, indoor/outdoor, occluded, etc.
  • Change deltas. Differences against a prior capture of the same site — what appeared, what disappeared, what moved.
  • Embeddings. Vector representations for nearest-neighbor evidence retrieval and clustering across captures.

Every output emits as a structured Thunderline event with a sealed Thundervine receipt — provenance bound to the source frame, the model version, and the operator scope under which inference ran.

Why "autonomous" is the right word here

The vision pipeline is autonomous. The outcomes go to humans.

"Autonomous" in WAVE refers to the pipeline, not the decision. There is no human-in-the-loop on perception — no operator clicks "OK, label this frame" before the event is emitted. But every WAVE output is consumed by something that does have a human in its loop. That discipline split is the whole product.

Autonomous · machine territory

The vision pipeline

Frames arrive. Models run. Detections, OCR strings, scene labels, change deltas, and embeddings are produced. Receipts are sealed. Events are published.

No operator approves the output of a YAM detection call before it leaves the worker. No reviewer confirms an OCR string before it is associated with its bbox. The pipeline runs continuously, headlessly, on every frame WAVE is asked to see.

→
Human-decided · operator territory

The downstream consumers

Every WAVE event reaches a downstream consumer that has a human in the loop. PROBATIO uses WAVE detections as candidate evidence that a reviewer accepts, rejects, or annotates. COCC rolls WAVE outputs into operational posture that a manager interprets. ATLAS pulls WAVE evidence to answer an operator's question.

No WAVE output, on its own, drives an outcome. A WAVE event is what a person reads, weighs, and signs against.

Autonomous vision; non-autonomous outcomes. The model runs without supervision. The decision, always, doesn't.

Where WAVE's outputs go

One vision substrate. Many downstream consumers.

WAVE doesn't render its own UI. WAVE doesn't run its own dashboard. WAVE emits events into the Thunderline substrate, and other services subscribe to those events according to their own concerns. Each consumer carries its own human-in-the-loop discipline.

PROBATIO

When a closeout package needs review, PROBATIO subscribes to WAVE detections and OCR strings tied to the workbook's site and time window. Extractions become candidate evidence. The human reviewer accepts, rejects, or annotates. The package seals a receipt either way.

COCC

For continuous operational posture, COCC aggregates WAVE outputs across crews, regions, and time. Which crews are trending up on missing-photo events. Which regions are over-indexed on needs-human dispositions. Posture is the read; the operator decides what to do.

ATLAS

When an operator asks ATLAS a question about a site — "what changed at tower 47-B between Tuesday and Friday?" — ATLAS pulls WAVE's change deltas and embeddings, summarises them with provenance, and presents the answer with the receipts that grounded it.

External consumers

Third-party systems subscribe to WAVE events through Thunderline event subscriptions — same contract, same receipts, same policy gate. A customer's existing GIS, EAM, or asset-management platform can be a first-class WAVE consumer without bespoke integration.

No direct HTTP between siblings. WAVE never calls PROBATIO. PROBATIO never calls WAVE. Every cross-service hop travels through the Thunderline event substrate, under PolicyGate authorization, with a Thundervine receipt sealed at the boundary. It's not a microservice mesh — it's a governed substrate with subscribed consumers.
The stack underneath

BEAM-native. Local-private. Buyer-deployable.

WAVE runs on the same runtime the rest of the Thunderline substrate runs on — Elixir on the BEAM. The model layer plugs in as an Ortex-loaded ONNX graph for the deterministic vision pieces (the YAM detection family), with a VisionPAC pool for the language-of-vision pieces — one or many PACs each running a different VLM, observing the same evidence in parallel. Why have one model looking at it when you can have many. All inference happens on the customer's host. No external API.

Component What it does
YAM YOLO Autonomous Model · Ortex / ONNX

Object detection. The YAM family of detection models — exported to ONNX, loaded into the BEAM through Ortex (the Elixir binding to ONNX Runtime) and served through yolo_elixir. Cabinets, routers, cables, meters, equipment-on-pallets, vehicles, crew presence — anything WAVE has been trained to recognise. Bounding boxes plus confidence scores, returned as native Elixir terms. YAM models are produced by Cerebros under the sibling-head training discipline; every inference references its training-receipt hash.
Evision OpenCV in Elixir

Bounding-box cropping, frame normalisation, basic image transforms. Takes the bbox coordinates emitted by YAM and produces the cropped tile that gets passed to OCR or to the VisionPAC pool for closer reading. Pure native call into OpenCV through a NIF, on the BEAM.
VisionPAC pool PACs dedicated to observing

The language-of-vision layer. A pool of Persistent Attentive Cognitions — one or many — each running a vision-language model and observing the cropped tiles in parallel. One PAC reads text. Another describes the scene. A third answers structured questions against the same image. The same evidence gets seen by multiple PACs at once; consensus and disagreement are themselves recorded in the receipt. All PACs run on the same host, reachable only over loopback. Never exposed beyond the local host; never reachable from the public internet. Why have one model looking at it when you can have many.
Bumblebee + CLIP Optional zero-shot layer

An optional layer for zero-shot scene classification when training a YAM head for a new vertical isn't yet warranted. CLIP-class embedding compares an image against a set of natural-language labels. Useful for fast new-vertical scaffolding before a fine-tuned YAM is delivered through Cerebros.
Elixir rules engine Deterministic match

Where vision output meets MOP (method-of-procedure) requirements. Did the detected cabinet match the cabinet specified in the workbook? Does the OCR string match the expected serial-number pattern? Pure deterministic Elixir — explicit branches, explicit failure modes, replayable. Models extract; rules decide what counts.
Local-private deployment Customer perimeter

The whole stack — YAM, Evision, the VisionPAC pool, Bumblebee, the rules engine, the receipt sealing — runs on the customer's host, inside the customer's perimeter. Worksite imagery does not leave that perimeter. There is no WAVE cloud. There is no external API. There is no third-party model vendor in the inference path.

WAVE is a BEAM-native pipeline because everything else in the ATLAS family is. Same runtime, same supervision tree, same backpressure semantics, same receipt machinery. Vision becomes another well-behaved component on the substrate — not a Python sidecar with its own operational story.

Six application examples

One substrate. Many verticals.

The WAVE pipeline is vertical-agnostic. The model heads, the rules, and the consuming services are what specialise. Below are six places the same WAVE substrate maps directly to a real operating workflow — non-exhaustive.

01

Telecom field-crew package screening

Closeout photos for a cell tower or fiber drop. WAVE detects required equipment, reads serial numbers, flags missing or mismatched photos. PROBATIO turns those detections into candidate evidence; the reviewer signs the package. Our first vertical.

02

Continuous worksite monitoring

Drone fleets running daily passes over an active build. WAVE produces change deltas between captures — what moved, what was added, what was removed — and feeds them into a posture view a project manager reads on Monday morning.

03

Insurance loss assessment imagery

Claims photos uploaded after a loss event. WAVE extracts the visible damage, reads any plates or serial numbers, and tags the scene. The adjuster reviews structured evidence alongside the live narrative — they still write the determination.

04

Industrial defect detection

Manufacturing line cameras streaming product imagery. WAVE flags defect candidates against a model head trained for the line's specific defect classes. A QA operator dispositions; the line decides whether to stop. Vision is the assist; humans hold the line.

05

Energy grid asset condition tracking

Pole-line, substation, and right-of-way imagery from helicopter or drone runs. WAVE tracks asset condition across captures — corrosion appearing, vegetation encroaching, hardware shifting. Asset managers decide which findings get a truck roll.

06

Construction daily-progress vision

Phone-camera and fixed-camera imagery from an active construction site. WAVE produces structured daily-progress observations — what got framed, what got delivered, what changed since yesterday — for the daily report a superintendent signs.

Cerebros training partnership

Audited training, audited inference, one chain.

WAVE's vision models are fine-tuned through Cerebros — a research-grade training discipline run as a sibling head with manifest-locked envelopes and sealed training receipts. The training receipt and the inference receipt chain together: the model was audited, and so is what it sees today.

Training side · Cerebros

Manifest-locked training

Cerebros runs the fine-tuning under a sealed manifest envelope: dataset hash, training config hash, model architecture, evaluation pass-criteria. The output of a Cerebros run is an ONNX model artifact plus a training receipt — content-addressable, replayable, and itself a Thundervine artifact.

→
Inference side · WAVE

Receipt-bound inference

WAVE loads the ONNX artifact as a YAM model via yolo_elixir + Ortex; VisionPAC-pool VLMs are loaded analogously into their PAC processes. Every inference WAVE emits references the training-receipt hash of the model that produced it. The inference receipt and the training receipt form one chain: customer can audit not just what the model said, but which model said it and how that model was made.

Your customer photos don't leave the perimeter; they get read by a model that was itself audited.

Cerebros is a research-grade training lab — a sibling head inside the substrate, not a vendor. The discipline is a separation: Cerebros is allowed to be experimental because WAVE will only load model artifacts whose training receipt is acceptable to the customer's policy. New model in development doesn't mean new model in production. Plan-acceptance is not implementation-authorization.

Buyer-safe by design

What WAVE is not.

We hold a tight scope. WAVE is a general-purpose worksite vision capability — we say what it is, and we say what it isn't, on the same page. The buyer should never be surprised in either direction.

  • Not a public-safety, 911, or emergency-response product. WAVE is positioned as a general real-time worksite vision capability. It is not deployed in life-safety paths. Use cases that require regulator-graded reliability are not WAVE use cases.
  • Not regulator-approved. No claim of approval, certification, or accreditation under any regulator's framework. Customers operating in regulated environments are responsible for their own compliance posture; WAVE supplies receipts they can use, not approvals they can rely on.
  • Not autonomous decisioning at the outcome level. The vision pipeline is autonomous; the outcomes are not. Every WAVE output reaches a downstream consumer with a human in the loop. WAVE never closes a ticket, signs a package, dispatches a truck, or denies a claim on its own.
  • Not for use cases the customer doesn't have authority to deploy in. If a customer doesn't have the operating authority — legal, contractual, or jurisdictional — to capture and process the imagery in question, WAVE doesn't fix that. The substrate enforces what the customer's policy allows. Authority is the customer's; the receipts make it visible.
  • Plan-acceptance is not implementation-authorization. A WAVE deployment plan landing in documentation is not a deployment authorization. Each move from plan to running pipeline is its own ruling, with its own receipt. We hold this discipline across every product in the family — it shows up here too.
